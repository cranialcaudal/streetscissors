**Goal:** Build single-leg calf strength and, just as importantly, expose the **Achilles tendon** to progressive load so it adapts and stiffens. The Achilles stores and returns elastic energy on every running stride; a tendon conditioned to that load propels you, while an unconditioned one becomes the season's most common breakdown point.

### Execution
Stand on one leg, ideally with the ball of the foot on the edge of a step so the heel can travel through a full range. Rise all the way up onto the ball of the foot, pause at the top, then lower **slowly and under control** until you feel a deep stretch through the calf and Achilles. The slow lowering — the eccentric phase — is the part that matters most; resist the urge to drop and bounce.

Do the work on each leg independently. Running and jumping are single-leg events, and training both sides separately keeps a stronger leg from masking a weaker one.

### Why it works
Tendon is living tissue that remodels in response to mechanical load. A narrative review of exercise effects on the Achilles found that progressive loading drives measurable adaptation in the tendon's biomechanical properties — increased stiffness and load tolerance — which is precisely what protects it under the repetitive strain of distance running (Yu et al., 2022).

The eccentric emphasis is not arbitrary. Alfredson's landmark protocol showed that heavy-load *eccentric* calf training resolved chronic Achilles tendinosis in recreational athletes who had otherwise been headed for surgery (Alfredson et al., 1998). That study reshaped tendon rehab around slow, loaded lowering — and the same stimulus that *rehabilitates* an injured Achilles is what *prophylactically* builds a resilient one. Doing it single-leg ensures each tendon gets the full dose.

### Programming notes
Prioritize full range and a slow (3–4 second) lowering over rep count. Start with bodyweight for 2–3 sets of 12–15 per leg; progress by adding load (hold a dumbbell) before adding reps. A straight knee biases the gastrocnemius; a slightly bent knee shifts emphasis toward the soleus, which carries much of the load in steady running — both are worth rotating in as volume climbs.
