**Goal:** Learn to keep the lower back flat and the deep core engaged while the arms and legs move independently — the foundation of dissociating limb movement from spinal movement.

### Execution
Lie on your back, arms reaching toward the ceiling, knees bent to 90° over the hips. Press your lower back firmly into the floor and hold it there. Slowly extend the opposite arm and leg until they hover just above the ground, return, and alternate. If the low back lifts off the floor, you've gone too far.

### Why it works
The dead bug is a supine anti-extension drill: as the limbs lengthen the lever arm, the deep abdominal wall must work to stop the pelvis tilting and the lumbar spine arching. It belongs to the family of abdominal exercises whose core-activation profiles have been measured directly (Escamilla et al., 2010), and it teaches the stabilizing role of the core in its safest possible position.

### Programming notes
3 x 6-10 per side, slow. The lower back must stay pressed to the floor the entire time — that contact is the success criterion. Reduce the range or keep the knees bent if you can't maintain it.
