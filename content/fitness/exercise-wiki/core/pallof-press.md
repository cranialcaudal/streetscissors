**Goal:** Train the trunk to *resist* rotation under load. This anti-rotation quality is what lets the core act as a rigid bridge that transfers power from the legs and hips into the throwing arm or the bat, instead of leaking that energy through an unwanted twist of the spine.

### Execution
Anchor a band (or set a cable) at roughly chest height. Stand side-on to the anchor so the line of pull wants to rotate you toward it. Interlace your hands over your sternum, brace your abdominals as if expecting a punch, and set your feet about shoulder-width apart with a soft bend in the knees.

Press the handle straight out from your chest until the arms are fully extended. The band is now pulling hard to rotate your torso toward the anchor — your job is to refuse. Keep the hips and shoulders square to the front and the ribs stacked over the pelvis. Hold for a beat at full extension, then return to the sternum under control. The arms only press and retract; *all of the real work is the trunk staying still.*

### Why it works
The core's primary job in athletic movement is not to crunch — it is to **stabilize the spine and transmit force** between the lower and upper body. Behm and colleagues, reviewing core training, describe the trunk musculature as functioning chiefly as stabilizers, and note that exercises demanding resistance to motion are what target that stabilizing role (Behm et al., 2010).

The Pallof press is deliberately a *standing* exercise, and that matters. McGill's analysis of torso exercises performed standing showed that this class of movement can impose a meaningful challenge on the trunk muscles while keeping spinal motion and compressive load relatively low, and that it trains the motor patterns used to brace and transfer load in upright, real-world postures (McGill et al., 2009). In other words: high training value for the anti-rotation pattern, low cost to the spine — exactly what you want from a movement you repeat year-round to protect a throwing shoulder and a hitting/pitching trunk.

### Programming notes
Use a band light enough that you can hold the squared position without your hips drifting or your spine rotating — form is the entire point, and a band that wins the fight defeats the exercise. Move slowly, breathe behind the brace (don't hold your breath), and treat the pause at full extension as the working portion. 2–3 sets of 8–12 controlled reps per side.
