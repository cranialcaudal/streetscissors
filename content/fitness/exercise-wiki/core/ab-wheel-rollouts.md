**Goal:** Train anti-extension — resisting the spine bending backward as the load tries to pull you into an arch. This is the bracing strength that keeps the trunk rigid when force passes through it.

### Execution
Kneel and grip the wheel under your shoulders. Set a slight posterior pelvic tilt (ribs down, glutes lightly squeezed) and roll forward, reaching the wheel away from you. Go only as far as you can while keeping the lower back from sagging into an arch, then pull yourself back by bracing the abs. The lower back position is the limit — not how far you can reach.

### Why it works
EMG comparisons of abdominal exercises place rollout-style movements among the highest for rectus abdominis and oblique activation (Escamilla et al., 2010). The demand is anti-extension: the abdominal wall fires hard to stop the lumbar spine from hyperextending as your base of support moves away from you. More broadly, the trunk musculature is trained here in its primary athletic role — as a stabilizer that resists motion (Behm et al., 2010).

### Programming notes
Let your ability to hold a neutral spine govern the range; a partial rollout done correctly beats a full one with a sagging back. 3 x 5-10, controlled out and back. Regress to a band-assisted or standing-wall version if the low back can't stay neutral.
