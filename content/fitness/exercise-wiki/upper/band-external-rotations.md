**Goal:** Strengthen the posterior rotator cuff — chiefly the **infraspinatus** and **teres minor** — the small muscles that externally rotate and stabilize the shoulder and, critically, that *decelerate* the arm after a throw. A durable throwing shoulder is built far more by what slows the arm down than by what speeds it up.

### Execution
Pin your working elbow tight to your side and bend it to 90°, so the forearm points straight ahead. Hold a light band with tension across the front of your body. Keeping the elbow glued to your ribs, rotate *only the forearm* outward, opening like a gate hinged at the elbow. Squeeze briefly at the end range, then return slowly against the band's pull.

Two cues make or break this: the elbow never leaves your side (tuck a rolled towel there if it helps), and the torso never twists to cheat the band open. The moment the shoulder shrugs or the body rotates, larger muscles have taken over and the cuff is no longer the target.

### Why it works
Electromyographic studies of the shoulder show that external-rotation exercises performed with the elbow at the side preferentially recruit the infraspinatus and teres minor while keeping deltoid activity comparatively low — meaning the load lands on the cuff itself rather than being stolen by the bigger surrounding muscles (Reinold et al., 2004). Follow-up EMG work on common rehabilitation exercises reinforced which positions best isolate the cuff versus letting the deltoid dominate, and is the basis for the strict "elbow pinned, no shrug" execution above (Reinold et al., 2007).

This is why external rotations are a prehab staple for throwers: the posterior cuff absorbs enormous eccentric load braking the arm during follow-through, and EMG-guided isolation lets you build that tissue specifically, before the volume of a season exposes any weakness.

### Programming notes
This is endurance and tissue-health work, not a max-strength lift — use a light band and higher reps (2–3 sets of 12–20), with a slow, controlled return on every rep. Best programmed as part of a warm-up or accessory block, not chased to failure. Run pull/cuff volume *ahead* of pressing volume to keep the shoulder balanced.
